On this blog over 10 years ago, I made [a rallying
cry][Don't Learn to Code, Learn to Program Instead - But Come Back In 10 Years]
for the field of computer programming to use richer media, to evolve its
expression beyond _text_.

(My most viral article. #1 on Hacker News for a day. Bless you, salacious
title.)

Then [I yearned for a productivity boost][Code As Craft As Career?]. Today's
moment in agentic coding might be what I was asking for. Even as I adopt AI's
higher leverage, I can't help feel wistful.

### Back to reality

Despite my rallying cry, I personally doubled down on the skill of pushing
around _text_. My programming workhorses to this day remain **the terminal and
Vim**. Given the programming medium we currently have, mastery of that medium
still compounds. See my [dotfiles] commit history for how much I've sharpened
that ax.

Pushing around plain text like via the terminal is precise. It's fast. It's
_fun_. When I pair, teammates are startled by the speed of it. It's the closest
ol' crusties like me get to the flow state of working with your hands on the
job. Typing uses your hands, of course, but not in the connected, intimate way
that you would bartending or woodworking all day, say. So the shortcuts and
chords of a TUI are the next best thing.

This addresses a couple of my crafter values.

1. Have fun.
2. From my dotfiles' README:
   > Use whatever makes you productive. Keep investing in and sharpening your
   > tools to **shorten the feedback loop**. The quicker it is to try an idea or
   > to cross reference it, the quicker it is to improve upon or discard.

Fun though it may be, for the arcane minutiae of coding, I don't necessarily
recommend beginners cling to 1970s tech like I do. But in the era of AI agents,
it's starting not to matter either way. The feedback loop is evolving into an
entirely different beast.

### Wake-up call

Around the time I resumed a full-time role in tech in 2025, the networking buzz
was all about Cursor, an AI-oriented VSCode fork. All my teammates were using
it. I feared, will I finally have to adopt VSCode?

VSCode never clicked for me. The persistent chrome and editor config inflicted
on the team felt intrusive. Why should a repo care what GUI I use? I wanted code
maximized, chrome hidden, and every mode switch reachable from home row. Every
mouse reach to cross reference something felt like death. Maybe Vim spoiled me.
VSCode's Vim emulation never came close.

### Revenge of the TUI

VSCode-alikes' under the covers are just calling tools. GUIs atop CLIs. Under
the covers, to format or compile your project, the IDEs are calling tools the
same way a human would in a terminal or your CI would in the cloud. Over the
years, IDEs have done some proprietary magic with their tool calling, to
different degrees. But the more IDEs adopted the Unix philosophy, the more
harmony, the more mindshare they got. The more proprietary magic, the more they
fall behind other editors.

In the Unix philosophy, what is the primary language tools interchange? _Text_.

You know what is good at dealing with large amounts of text? LLMs.

The interactive, AI-assisted autocomplete that was so nice suddenly started to
look like a bottleneck. What if we instead let agents run wild on even more text
than what the human was focused on? A text-based interface stopped seeming
retro.

Enter a slew of TUIs like Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, and even Cursor's alongside
its VSCode GUI. The LLM-based agents are just inputting and outputting text and
shelling out to tools. Why launch a heavyweight GUI if you're just going to push
text around via an agent?

The appearance of these TUIs let me drop the VSCode-alike. I could arrange my
terminal splits in a way that mimicked the Cursor GUI's split editor and AI chat
panel. Better than a mimic, I could leap between the splits in the way that Vim
and terminal splits long made sense and felt unified to _me_, building off
compounded fluency.

### Subweapons

A couple side shoutouts that made agentic adoption in the terminal even better
around this time. While not strictly necessary to work in the AI-assisted coding
era, I would recommend the following to any aspiring terminal user, especially
whose rate of pushing text is increasing.

#### Ghostty

Switching from iTerm to [Ghostty][Ghostty] made high-output agentic TUIs feel
smoother and more legible, especially when diffs are flying by and I am
scrolling back through long chat sessions.

The default config is solid: compare my [iTerm config][iTerm Config] (36K of
.plist XML) and [Ghostty config][Ghostty Config] (3 optional lines of text).

Ghostty is a minimal, drop-in replacement for stock Terminal.app or a basic
iTerm install. If however you want a little more out of the box from a terminal,
try [Warp][Warp], which has AI built in. The fact Silicon Valley is investing in
terminals reassures me about this ancient tech.[^2]

#### Jujutsu

AI means more edits, diffs, branches, and commits to share with your team. I
assume you're already using version control to tell coherent, distinct stories
to your teammates, and need to integrate their changes in turn.

Switching from Git `git` to [Jujutsu][Jujutsu] `jj` made it easier to reorder,
squash, rebase, and stack those changes into a clean story for code review,
while minimizing conflict and interoperating with Git. Git's UX conflates these
concepts, while unwinding stacked changes leads to repeated, self-inflicted
conflicts. `jj` [gives proper UX attention to these
operations][Jujutsu Strategies] and minimizes such conflicts.

`jj`'s automatic snapshotting also rescued me from the Cursor GUI chat panel's
wonky undo states. In Cursor, it was often unclear what `Cmd+Z` would undo, what
Vim's `u` would undo, and what rewinding checkpoints within the thread would do
to the actual working copy. Switching to the terminal for edit, AI chat, and
code review, `jj` adds a safety net. Since I am already `jj diff`-ing what the
agent changed, `jj` is quietly snapshotting repo state, as it does on many `jj`
commands. That made `jj undo` a decent way to rewind. Although `jj undo` is not
a substitute for deliberate commits.

### Hardcore mode

I'm not the most hardcore I could be with the terminal. My hands _do_ leave home
row. I do use a mouse, to scroll or copy text. I'm also not the most hardcore I
could be with AI-assisted coding. On the [Steve Yegge scale of AI
adoption][Welcome to Gas Town], the Vim + terminal setup I've described so far,
I'm only on stage 3-4 out of 8.

> Stage 3: **Agent in IDE, YOLO mode:** Trust goes up. You turn off permissions,
> agent gets wider.
>
> Stage 4: **In IDE, wide agent:** Your agent gradually grows to fill the
> screen. Code is just for diffs.

You could argue whether I'm in an "IDE" with Vim or not. That doesn't matter.
The width is the interesting observation.

Cursor foresaw this evolution of programmers. It forced an update in late 2025
that made the width of its AI chat panel default to wider than the code editor,
calling the newly wider panel "Agent Mode." Most users were used to the editor
being wider, like in the earlier images in this article. Users complained, which
convinced the Cursor team to revert. But Cursor was onto something. The
enlightened AI-assisted programmer cares less and less about the code viewer.
The more you trust the AI, the more you should focus on good interaction with
the agent. Anything else is bottlenecks where the human gets in the way.

Peter Steinberger suggested getting away from these GUI facades around the AI,
to [just talk to it][Just Talk to It], one of the most inspiring AI-adoption
journey articles I've read. Treat the AI model less like a feature inside an IDE
and more like the primary interface. Widen the interaction with the agent.
Shrink the ornamentation and harnessing around the agent responses.

Inspired by Steinberger, you might advance to Yegge stages 5-6.

> Stage 5: **CLI, single agent. YOLO.** Diffs scroll by. You may or may not look
> at them.
>
> Stage 6: **CLI, multi-agent, YOLO.** You regularly use 3 to 5 parallel
> instances. You are very fast.

The terminal splits no longer mimic VSCode, but become something else:

> Stage 7: **10+ agents, hand-managed.** You are starting to push the limits of
> hand-management.
>
> Stage 8: **Building your own orchestrator.** You are on the frontier,
> automating your workflow.

Perhaps agent orchestration is the productivity multiplier I yearned for, all
those years ago. It is no longer the woodworking-like, hands-on flow state I
described at the beginning of the article. That you use even use the terminal,
or carefully crafted code or commit history? In the agentic frontier, maybe
those don't matter.

### Vibe engineering

That anybody with a modest computer can be a vibe coder now, the special
hands-on skills I invested so much time in? I can't help feel a loss. But I'm
hopeful engineering discipline still offers some expertise to the world.

First, some reassurance from the fact that everybody is on a different stage of
their AI adoption journey, software engineer or not. In Silicon Valley and on
tech social media, the cutting edge discussion buzz makes it seem like everybody
is at least Yegge stage 5. Technology is quick to change. People are slow to
change. Zooming out of the Silicon Valley echo chamber, I would guess what's hot
in the valley hasn't reached SMBs, where many software engineers are still
stages 1-2. Most _people_ are stages 0-1 ("I chatted with AI a couple years ago
and it sucked"). We all know somebody who hasn't even tried generative AI. In
tech, we lose sight of that.

Good as the agents have gotten in the last several months at tool calling and
one-off scripts, I can't trust them without processes yet, or I'd be fired.
Well, it hasn't been said in those words, but: if I can barely explain what I
shipped to production, to myself or to my team, and there's a big regression,
the business is going to be in trouble and I'm going to be in trouble. With
hobby projects, I've vibed, turned my engineer mind off, and lost connection
with what the AI actually built me. Simon Willison lists [these seemingly
evergreen engineering practices][Vibe Engineering] to keep the AI in line. They
will amplify already good engineers. Meanwhile a truly vibes-only project will
go off the rails, and collapse under its own tech debt weight.

If you're feeling like AI must be a 10x coding multiplier and you don't know how
to reach that number, remember engineering team constraints, like the above
evergreen practices, do not disappear just because code is cheaper to produce.
["10x output" claims often ignore non-coding
bottlenecks][AI 10x Imposter Syndrome]. If anything, team constraints become
more obvious: review quality, shared understanding, operational safety, and
coordination. A 10x claim while retaining these processes seems unrealistic.

Could a team _without_ those engineering processes safely, robustly leapfrog
teams with? While I can haughtily dismiss the new generation of tools and tool
wielders as not knowing what they're doing, such a dismissal does eerily sound
like the cycle of tech: someone doing it new, different, cheaper, and taking
over. From my article yearning for a productivity boost:

> Most consumers though will settle for cheap and highly available. Economics
> wins.
>
> …
>
> The technology that ultimately matters is not some hotly debated contender.
> What matters is the technology where you forget what the old way of doing
> things even was.
>
> Did you know there was a typesetter profession?

The future is here, it's just not evenly distributed yet.

Part of me wanted this better way of expressing programming to the computer.
Back in 2014, I was already asking for a mode of creation more powerful than
hand-writing text for every last detail. AI orchestration may be the closest
thing yet to that wish. I like the extra leverage. I like the speed. I like that
the terminal and other text-first tools I invested in still map surprisingly
okay onto this new world. I also feel a little sad that one of the most
intimate, hands-on parts of the job may matter less.

I get to keep my hands on home row, at least for one more turn.
