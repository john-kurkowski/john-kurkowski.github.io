I'll shout out a few tools that made [my AI adoption in the
terminal][Hands on Home Row for AI-Assisted Coding] even better.

That previous article is a memoir that hopefully captures an evergreen feeling
and narrative. Meanwhile, any article listing tools might be outdated by the
next round of AI models. For an improved AI model that knows how to discover how
to do a task autonomously, additional harnessing might just get in the way, per
[the Bitter Lesson][The Bitter Lesson]. Especially if the tools
[diverge][Choose Boring Technology] from what the majority of people use, and
therefore from the majority of AI training data.

Anyway, I'm one of those rebels that sharpens my own tools, not the majority's
tools. At my own peril.

While the following tools are not strictly necessary to work in the AI-assisted
coding era, I would recommend them to any aspiring terminal user, especially
whose rate of pushing text is increasing.

### Ghostty

Switching from iTerm to [Ghostty][Ghostty] made high-output agentic TUIs feel
smoother and more legible, especially when diffs are flying by and I am
scrolling back through long chat sessions.

The default config is solid: compare my [iTerm config][iTerm Config] (36K of
.plist XML) and [Ghostty config][Ghostty Config] (3 optional lines of text).

Ghostty is a minimal, drop-in replacement for stock Terminal.app or a basic
iTerm install. If however you want a little more out of the box from a terminal,
try [Warp][Warp], which has AI built in. The fact Silicon Valley is investing in
terminals reassures me about this ancient tech.

Speaking of AI, in preparing this article, I also enjoyed reading Ghostty
creator [Mitchell Hashimoto's AI adoption journey][My AI Adoption Journey]. For
example, I was struck by deliberate use of agents, especially at end-of-day,
rather than running them 24/7 for its own sake.

### Jujutsu

AI means more edits, diffs, branches, and commits to share with your team. I
assume you're already using version control to [tell coherent, distinct
stories][Avoid Git First Drafts] to your teammates, and need to integrate their
changes in turn.

Switching from Git `git` to [Jujutsu][Jujutsu] `jj` made it easier to reorder,
squash, rebase, and stack those changes into a clean story for code review,
while minimizing conflict and interoperating with Git. Git's UX conflates these
concepts, while unwinding stacked changes leads to repeated, self-inflicted
conflicts. `jj` [gives proper UX attention to these
operations][Jujutsu Strategies] and minimizes such conflicts.

`jj`'s automatic snapshotting also rescued me from the Cursor GUI chat panel's
wonky undo states. In Cursor, it was often unclear what `Cmd+Z`/`u` would undo,
and what rewinding checkpoints within the chat thread would do to the actual
working copy. Switching to the terminal for edit, AI chat, and code review, `jj`
adds a safety net. Since I am already `jj diff`-ing what the agent changed, `jj`
is quietly snapshotting repo state, as it does on many `jj` commands. That made
`jj undo` a decent way to rewind.

Although neither Cursor GUI undo states nor `jj undo` are substitutes for
deliberate commits, and you can get an agent to help with commits too. If the
agent isn't going to stomp on my human changes, like in its own worktree, I let
it commit at will. I can clean up its commits later. [Here are my agent
instructions for when I tell it to use worktrees][AGENTS.md] (workspaces in `jj`
parlance).

In my previous article, I really enjoyed reading [Just Talk To It], but the
author Peter Steinberger is not a fan of worktrees, and lets his parallel agents
commit directly to `main`. I sense the author's learning project at the time was
a solo effort, he didn't have to coordinate with an engineering team. At my
company, I still have to linearize my changes for review by another human, at
least for now. `jj` eases this process.

### "Which model do you use?"

Ah, the burning question at programming networking. The modern day Emacs vs.
Vim, spaces vs. tabs. And so the least evergreen section of this article.

I side with Steinberger: since GPT-5.2 in October 2025, the GPT model series has
felt stellar, like a clone of me, a senior software developer. Its Rust-based
[Codex](https://chatgpt.com/codex) TUI has been snappy for a long time. I for
sure have to steer the model. But I have to delete a lot less than with Claude
Code, whose output is more of a junior developer. It's less evergreen, with a
lot of "Why is this comment here?" to the detriment of maintainability.

Claude Code is the darling of the software developer world. Naturally, I opted
for the less popular tool, Codex. I wouldn't be writing this section if I
weren't a rebel.

> There are only two kinds of languages: the ones people complain about and the
> ones nobody uses.
>
> —Bjarne Stroustrup

Claude and Claude Code do have very pleasant interfaces. They have first mover's
advantage, so there are lots of features. If you're just getting started in the
terminal, you will enjoy. I also admire Claude's ability to move between
devices, and even launch a custom web \*gasp\* GUI one-shotted for you.

But if you're over the chat pleasantries, the agent blowing smoke, and need a
decisive technician in your terminal, I've really enjoyed GPT-5.2 through 5.4.
It gets to the point and produces higher quality, shorter code.
